About Me

       I am a postdoctoral fellow from the Epidemiology Branch, National Institute of Environmental Health Sciences, National Institute of Health. I have a very strong personal interest and passion as well as adequate knowledge, and research experience on developing risk scores or indexes, predictive models, and spatial maps using data science methods including machine learning (ML) models, spatial-temporal analysis, remote sensing techniques, and geostatistical analysis. Most of my research had utilized large or spatial datasets, such as NASA, EPA, NOAA, US Census, American Community Surveys, etc. and linked these environmental and social datasets with NY statewide emergency department (ED)/ hospitalization data (over 140 million cases) and Gulf Long Term Follow-Up Study across entire U.S. at the individual level. I have a lot of experience in various epidemiologic study designs (cross-sectional, ecological, cohort, case-control, case-crossover, and clinical trials), and developing strategies to handle various biases in epidemiologic studies. I served as statistician and participated in several NIH- and EPA-funded grants regarding power outages, PM composition, ultrafine particles, meteorological conditions, and school environments. In addition, I built several online predictive tools for COVID-19, congenital heart diseases, and enteral nutrition intolerance. I also built a two-stage downscaling model incorporating ML models and Bayesian spatial temporal models to refine meteorological exposures from 27 km to 1 km. My current work focuses on utilizing environmental and social datasets to build comprehensive indices to predictive aging-related outcomes such as depression, cognitive functions, cardiovascular diseases, and neurological diseases.
